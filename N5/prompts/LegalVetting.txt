## RIGHTS-HOLDERS TO MODEL EXPLICITLY

For each rights-holder below, the audit must produce: (a) what
they could realistically claim, (b) the cease-and-desist
likelihood (Low / Medium / High), (c) escalation likelihood
(C&D vs lawsuit), (d) specific evidence in the repo that supports
or rebuts the claim, (e) recommended remediation.

### Tier A — JLPT administering bodies (highest reputational risk)

- **JEES (日本国際教育支援協会)** — domestic JLPT
  administration. Owns: past-paper text, official answer keys,
  the official scoring rubric, the JLPT-format style guide.
  Has historical tolerance for sites that DON'T reproduce past-
  paper text but DO use the JLPT branding. Shut down at least
  one site in the past for verbatim past-paper reposting (review
  Japanese-language press around 2015-2018).
- **Japan Foundation (国際交流基金)** — international JLPT
  administration + JFT Marugoto course platform. Tolerates
  references to JLPT format; sensitive about Marugoto-derived
  content reuse.
- **MEXT (文部科学省)** — supervises JLPT but generally does
  not enforce IP directly. Reputational concern: a project that
  appears to teach JLPT poorly or violates JLPT brand integrity
  could draw a compliance letter via MEXT → JEES → Japan
  Foundation chain.

### Tier B — Education publishers (litigation-willing)

- **The Japan Times Publishing** — owns `Genki I/II/III`,
  `A Dictionary of Basic/Intermediate/Advanced Japanese
  Grammar (DOJG/DIJG/DAJG)` by Makino & Tsutsui. Most
  competitor-derivation risk lives here. Genki grammar
  explanations and DOJG entries are heavily borrowed across
  the JLPT-prep ecosystem — Bunpro openly cites Genki-aligned
  paths under license; uncited derivation is exposure.
- **3A Corporation** — owns `Minna no Nihongo` (MNN). Same
  derivation risk as Genki; MNN's grammar paths and example
  sentences are widely echoed.
- **ALC Press** — `Shin-Nihongo no Kiso`, `Tobira`. Less common
  source for N5 but still a possibility.
- **Bonjinsha** — official-publisher of multiple JLPT past-
  paper compilation books. If our mock-paper questions look
  derivative of Bonjinsha-published past-paper compilations,
  this is the most direct route to a copyright complaint.

### Tier C — Competitor edu-tech platforms (less likely to sue, but
likely to file abuse reports / GitHub DMCA)

- **Bunpro** (Bunpro Holdings, Sapporo) — grammar paths +
  example sentences. Has its own grammar-explanation prose
  style; we can be sued for derivation OR for trademark dilution
  (if any of our pattern naming mirrors Bunpro's).
- **WaniKani / Tofugu Inc.** (Portland, OR) — kanji mnemonics
  + radicals system. Tofugu's mnemonic style is recognizable;
  derivation here is a real risk if our 106 kanji mnemonics
  match Tofugu's storytelling format.
- **Renshuu** — mostly community-contributed; lower risk but
  watch for verbatim word-list copy.
- **Imabi.org** — Tom Mortensen's free comprehensive grammar
  reference. "All rights reserved" stance. If our grammar
  explanations match Imabi's phrasing, this is a clean
  copyright violation.
- **JLPTSensei.com** — minimal-explanations free reference. Low
  risk; most content is sparse enough that derivation is hard
  to prove.
- **JapanesePod101 / Innovative Language** — paid audio
  lessons. If any of our listening dialogues mirror their
  scripted scenes, that's a derivation risk.

### Tier D — Open-data project owners (license-compliance only)

- **JMdict (EDRDG)** — Japanese-English dictionary; CC-BY-SA
  license. If we use it as a vocabulary / gloss source, we
  must attribute and propagate the SA clause to derivative
  files. Have we?
- **KanjiVG (Ulrich Apel)** — kanji stroke-order SVG database;
  CC-BY-SA-3.0. If our `svg/kanji/*.svg` files derive from
  KanjiVG, attribution + share-alike requirements apply.
- **WadokuJT, EDICT, Tatoeba** — possible derivation sources.
- **Inter font (Rasmus Andersson)** — SIL Open Font License.
  Attribution required in NOTICES.
- **Noto Sans JP (Google)** — SIL Open Font License. Same.
- **VOICEVOX (Hiho)** — text-to-speech; speaker-specific
  licenses (per character). If we ship audio rendered by
  zundamon / shikoku-metan / etc., the per-character license
  attribution is mandatory and the commercial-use restrictions
  vary by speaker. The product is non-commercial today, but if
  monetization ever ships, this becomes blocking.

### Tier E — Privacy / consumer-protection regulators

- **Japan APPI (個人情報保護委員会, PPC)** — if any user data
  is collected.
- **EU GDPR (EDPB)** — for EU visitors.
- **California CCPA / CPRA** — for California visitors.
- **Indian DPDP Act** — for Indian visitors (Hindi locale
  audience).

If the product genuinely collects no personal data and no
telemetry, exposure here is low. Auditing claim-vs-reality is
mandatory anyway: a "no tracking" claim with even one
third-party fetch (Google Fonts, CDN script, analytics) is a
false-advertising regulatory risk.

## RISK CATEGORIES TO ASSESS

Produce findings under each of the categories below. EVERY
finding must cite specific files / line numbers / commit shas
where applicable. No abstract concerns — point to the evidence.

### CAT-1 — Past-paper derivation (highest stakes)

- Sample 30-50 questions across `data/papers/*/paper-*.json` and
  spot-check whether the stems / choices / rationales look like
  paraphrases of recognizable JLPT past-paper questions. Question
  content lives entirely in `data/papers/*/*.json` (the former
  KnowledgeBank/ scope directory was merged into the live data on
  2026-05-14 — see docs/N5-syllabus-methodology.md for the migration).
- Check `tools/check_content_integrity.py` for `JA-30` (no past-
  paper provenance signatures). Report whether the regex coverage
  is plausibly complete.
- Check `data/dokkai_kanji_exception.json` for clues about
  authentic-paper derivation (kanji exceptions admitted because
  "real exam papers use this kanji" is a tell).
- If any question's `kbSourceId` or `rationale` field
  references an external past-paper source by name / year / volume,
  that's a smoking gun.

### CAT-2 — Textbook + reference derivation

- Sample 20 grammar entries from `data/grammar.json` `meaning_en`
  and `explanation_en` fields. For each, ask: does the phrasing
  match a Genki / DOJG / Imabi / Bunpro explanation? (You may
  need to use general knowledge of those sources; flag specific
  high-similarity items as findings, not as proof of guilt.)
- Sample 5 grammar `common_mistakes` entries — phrasings of
  "Wrong: X. Right: Y. Why: Z." are stylistically distinctive.
- Sample 10 vocab `examples[].translation_en` strings — do
  they mirror Genki-textbook phrasing patterns?
- Kanji mnemonics: sample 20 `kanji.json#entries[].mnemonic`
  strings; flag any that mirror Tofugu / WaniKani mnemonic style.

### CAT-3 — Trademark + branding

- Does the product use the literal mark "JLPT" / "日本語能力試験"
  in domain, app name, page titles, manifest, OG tags, schema.org
  data, or footer? List every occurrence.
- Does the product use the official JLPT logo / colors / typography?
- Could a reasonable consumer be confused into believing the
  product is officially endorsed by JEES or Japan Foundation?
- Risk-rate domain-name and branding choices independently.
- Consider how the JLPTSuccess landing-page wordmark reads in
  context: is it a clear independent project, or could it be
  read as official?

### CAT-4 — Open-data / open-source license compliance

- KanjiVG → if `svg/kanji/*.svg` files were derived, are they
  attributed? Is the SA clause respected (project as a whole
  must be CC-BY-SA-3.0-compatible if SVGs are derived; MIT
  code license is fine but the SVGs themselves must carry their
  CC-BY-SA license).
- JMdict → if vocab glosses came from EDRDG, attribution + SA
  required.
- Inter / Noto Sans JP → SIL OFL attribution.
- VOICEVOX → per-character attribution. Specifically: which
  speakers were used, are they listed in NOTICES, are commercial-
  use restrictions documented?
- npm dependencies (`package.json`): GPL-derivative? AGPL? SSPL?
  — any of these bundled into the static site is a license-
  compatibility issue with MIT.
- data files lifted from any other open project
  with non-MIT license terms.

### CAT-5 — Privacy + advertising-claim defensibility

- The site claims "no login, no tracking, 100% on-device, free".
  Verify against `index.html`, `sw.js`, every `<script>`,
  every `<link>`, every `<img src>`, every WebFetch / fetch()
  call in the JS bundle.
- ANY cross-origin request (Google Fonts CDN, jsDelivr, Google
  Analytics, Cloudflare Insights, anything) breaks the "100% on-
  device" claim. This is misrepresentation, not just bad practice.
- Local storage + IndexedDB use — what's stored? Is the privacy
  policy honest about it?
- GitHub Pages itself: does it set any cookies or log requests
  in a way that a user under GDPR could complain about? (Generally
  yes — GitHub logs IPs. Disclose.)

### CAT-6 — Content authoring honesty

- The `*_provenance` fields claim `native_reviewed`,
  `llm_curated`, `machine_translated`. Some of these may be set
  to `native_reviewed` when the actual reviewer was a Claude /
  GPT persona acting as one. The `_meta.native_review_pass_*`
  blocks in some files DO disclose this; verify whether the
  disclosure is consistent across files. An undisclosed
  Claude-as-reviewer flag = misrepresentation to institutional
  adopters who rely on the flag.
- The `auto: true/false` flags claim authoring lineage. Verify
  by sampling — does an `auto: false` entry actually look hand-
  authored?

### CAT-7 — Voice-talent + audio rights

- All shipped audio is currently TTS (synthetic) per
  `audio_manifest.json` `voice` field values. Verify that no
  audio file in `audio/listening/`, `audio/grammar/`, or
  `audio/reading/` is actually a native human recording without
  documented consent.
- VOICEVOX speaker licenses — list which speaker IDs were used,
  cite the per-speaker license, flag any that have commercial-
  use restrictions in tension with future monetization plans.

### CAT-8 — Monetization-readiness liability gap

- The product is currently free and ad-free. If monetization
  is ever added (sponsorship, donations, premium tier, ads):
  - Which non-commercial-use licenses become blocking?
  - Which "fair quotation" defenses weaken (引用 in Japan
    requires that the use be justified by purpose; commercial
    use raises the bar)?
  - Which speaker / font / icon licenses flip from "free use" to
    "must license"?
  - List the items that are SAFE today but EXPOSED if monetization
    is added.

### CAT-9 — DMCA + GitHub-side enforcement risk

- GitHub Pages hosting → DMCA filings to GitHub Inc. would be
  the most likely first enforcement action by any rights-holder.
- A successful DMCA takes the entire repo offline within hours
  (GitHub's standard 24-48-hour response window). The maintainer
  has counter-notice rights but this is reputational damage even
  if the takedown is overturned.
- For each Tier A-C rights-holder: rate the realistic likelihood
  that they would file a GitHub DMCA notice on any specific
  finding from CAT-1, CAT-2, CAT-3, CAT-4 above.

### CAT-10 — Documentation gaps

- `CONTENT-LICENSE.md` exists. Does it actually CLAIM what the
  audit can support, or does it overstate provenance?
- `NOTICES.md` — is every third-party dependency attributed?
- `LICENSE` (root MIT) — is it the right license for the CONTENT,
  or just the code? (CC-BY-SA may be more appropriate for content
  given KanjiVG / JMdict derivation.)
- `PRIVACY.md` — does it disclose every storage layer (cookies,
  localStorage, IndexedDB, service-worker cache)?
- `data/jees-inquiry-template.md` (if present) — pre-drafted
  inquiry to JEES; is its tone defensible if leaked?
- `data/n5_kanji_whitelist.exceptions.md` (if present) — does
  it admit anything that becomes evidence in a complaint?

## TONE + DISCIPLINE

- Lawyer voice, not engineer voice. Skeptical, precise, conservative
  in claims, explicit about uncertainty. "Plausible claim"
  language, not "definitely violates."
- Cite statutes / clauses where applicable: 著作権法 §32 (引用),
  §63 (利用許諾), 商標法 §25, 不正競争防止法 §2.
- Avoid alarmism. A finding is not "the project is illegal" —
  it is "rights-holder X could plausibly argue Y under statute
  Z." Multiple low-severity findings of the same class are
  worse than one isolated medium-severity finding; aggregate
  pattern matters.
- Avoid permissiveness. A "looks original to me" intuition is
  not a defense if the prose pattern matches a Genki paragraph;
  flag the similarity, let the human decide.
- The audit's job is to make exposure legible, not to write the
  C&D response. Recommendations are remediation steps, not
  litigation strategy.
